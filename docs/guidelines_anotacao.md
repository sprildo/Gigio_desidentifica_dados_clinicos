## Annotation Guidelines and Training Methodology (NER)

This document details the corpus construction process, entity annotation rules, and the Active Learning cycle used to train the pt_desidentificacao_ner model. The data used in this project were extracted from electronic health records of a single tertiary hospital in the state of São Paulo.

## 1. Corpus Composition and Data Extraction

To ensure the model generalized well across different hospital text formats, data extraction encompassed multiple sources, taking random samples of medical records from different specialties in both emergency room and inpatient settings. Records extracted included: `case summaries`, `diagnostic and therapeutic plans`,
`contact records`, `conduct notes`, and `provenance`. Prior analysis indicated that these records had a higher occurrence of identifiers.

The annotation was performed prioritizing sensitivity in the identification of Protected Health Information (PHI). The annotation was carried out by a single medical professional using the following rules:
1) Mark only proper `NAMES` (patients, doctors, nurses, and family members), excluding pronouns of address and professional titles (e.g., Dr., Mrs., etc.).
2) Mark `HOSPITAL RECORDS` that were not handled by regular expressions (hospital records are standardized and easily treatable by regex).
3) `DOCUMENTS`: Any type of records such as CRM, Corem, CPF, etc. were annotated, especially the hospital's exam numbers.
4) `ADDRESS`: The continuous block was marked.
5) `INSTITUTION`: Names of hospitals, clinics, and healthcare units; if the text only cited the municipality name, it was marked as CITY.
6) `CITY`: Municipalities of birth, provenance, or transfer.
7) `TELEPHONE`: Telephone contact numbers that were not previously captured by regular expressions (Regex).

The model was not taught to "correct" the text. False records generated by typos (e.g., anos,0001111A) were not marked with entities.


Some entities were rarely included in the texts, and even attempts to find data to reinforce these identifiers did not demonstrate their existence. The cities primarily refer to the hospital's macro-region. The medical records are exclusively medical and did not contain outpatient visits. These data reinforce the need for fine-tuning the model depending on its intended use.


## 2. Text Pre-processing and Cleaning


During the validation phase, it was proven that cleaning the raw hospital text improves entity extraction by spaCy. The model was trained on texts that underwent the following standardizations:
a) Conversion of all text to lowercase.
b) Removal or replacement of formatting characters that generate noise (removal of quotes, replacement of parentheses with spaces).
c) Reduction of repetitive characters (e.g., ___, ===, >>>) that frequently break tokenization.
d) Normalization of multiple whitespaces, tabs, and excessive line breaks.

The exact code used in this step is available in the preprocessamento.py script.


## 3. Annotation Methodology (Prodigy Workflow)
Training utilized the Prodigy tool (v1.18.5), following an Active Learning workflow structured in three phases:
1. Seed Batch (Cold Start): 100% manual annotation of 301 records. This phase ensured the introduction of annotation rules without the bias of a pre-trained model.
2. Assisted Annotation (Human-in-the-loop): Transition to review mode, where intermediate models pre-annotated the texts.
3. Batch Fusion (Cumulative Training): At each new cycle, the annotation datasets and assisted reviews were concatenated and trained from scratch, using data isolation to avoid double annotation of already reviewed records.


## 4. Annotated Data
A total of 3,195 records were analyzed, of which 2,555 had at least one manual annotation. The annotations included 8,781 names, 1,290 cities, 1,090 documents (basically exam numbers), 787 institutions (basically from the hospital's macro-region), 316 phones, 26 hospital records, and 0 addresses.
